Picture a company that pays a dividend, but not out of profits. It pays by printing brand-new shares and handing them to shareholders who show up to collect. Show up, and you end up with more shares. Sit out, and you still own the same number, but now they're a thinner slice of a bigger pie.

A staking reward is almost exactly that, and the stake for you is direct: it's the difference between "7% yield" being real income and being an illusion you're partly funding yourself. So here's the route: first where the new tokens actually come from, then who quietly pays for them, then how to tell whether the yield is worth it.

When you stake SOL, the network mints fresh SOL and routes it to stakers as a reward. Picture a clean example: 1,000 SOL exists, the network issues 70 new ones this year, and you hold 100 staked. You collect 7. You're up to 107 of a now-1,070 supply, the same share you started with. The holder who *didn't* stake still has their 100, but of a bigger pie. Their slice just thinned. The tokens weren't taken from anyone's pocket; they were *created*, and the bill is paid in dilution by everyone who sits out. Stake, and you're collecting the new shares. Sit out, and you're the one quietly funding them.
