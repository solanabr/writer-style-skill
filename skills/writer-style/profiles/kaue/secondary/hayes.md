# Writing Craft Profile — distilled from Arthur Hayes

> **Lane — engagement & stamina.** *Carry the reader through length on one organizing metaphor:* recast
> invisible forces as actors with motives, walk a round-number example, restate the stake, land on "what to
> watch / how you'd know I'm wrong." **Leads** macro / markets / geopolitics / tokenomics / crowd-psychology
> pieces. Anything where reader *abandonment* across length is the main risk.

*Captures the **transferable craft** that lets him pull a general reader through six thousand words on a
dry, technical subject: how he engages, sustains, and lands. It is **not** a voice impersonation: applied
in YOUR voice to produce ORIGINAL work. His persona (profanity and crude imagery, recurring nicknames, the
autobiographical trader identity, song-title sign-offs, anti-fiat ideology) is deliberately excluded — see
the note at the end.*

**What to lift from him specifically:** **engagement and stamina**, the load-bearing organizing metaphor
that becomes the spine of a long piece, the signposting and decomposition that make length feel easy, and
the stakes that pull a reader through the dry middle. The craft below is the menu; pull what serves the
lesson. (His native domain is economics/markets/tokenomics, but the engagement engine works on any long
or cold-audience piece.)

---

## 1. Engagement & narrative (his core strength)
- **The concrete-scene-as-organizing-metaphor — make it the SPINE, not a flourish.** Open on a vivid,
  specific scene from an everyday domain (a craft, a meal, a game, a hazard he's lived), extract its
  governing principle in a plain sentence, then pivot that principle onto the technical topic. And *keep
  returning to the same metaphor* as the structural backbone. He goes all the way: the metaphor governs the
  structure. It can title the piece, recurs at each turn, and names the final section. (Avalanche science → a buried "persistent
  weak layer" → the geopolitical fault under markets; nightclub "bottle rules" → who's in the
  *denominator* that pays → the closing section titled "The Denominator"; control the kite before the
  board → control the *quantity* of money before its price.) The reader grasps the *shape* before any
  jargon arrives, and the recurring image is the handrail that carries attention across length.
- **The stakes escalator — a concrete second-person consequence, right after the hook.** Answer "why
  should I keep reading?" with something that happens *to the reader*, their money, their project, their
  risk, never abstract importance. State it as the difference between two outcomes ("the difference
  between '7% yield' being real income and an illusion you're funding yourself"). That stake is the fuel
  through the dry middle; re-state it whenever the material gets technical. Frame it as *upside at risk for
  a builder*, never as a predator coming for the reader. The consequence is real, the tone stays warm.
- **Name the payoff debt across a digression.** When the opening scene runs long, say so and promise the
  return ("I know you don't care about my kitesurfing — but I promise there's a point; more on that in a
  bit"). Flagging the debt openly, then paying it, buys patience the digression would otherwise spend.
- **The callback bookend.** Close by returning to the opening scene, now recontextualized by everything
  learned. It signals completion and makes the lesson stick (end on the matcha and the cherry blossoms
  the meal was preparing you for).

## 2. Make complexity vivid and clear
- **Concretize the abstraction as an actor with a motive.** Recast an impersonal force or system as an
  agent doing something *for a reason* ("the network mints new tokens to pay validators," "the scheduler
  hands the transaction off to…," "the protocol *wants* fees to land here"). Give it a desire and the
  causality rides along for free. Agents-with-goals are far easier to track than aggregates.
- **Toy-number worked example with clean round numbers.** Run one small, made-up scenario all the way
  through with round figures, walking each consequence a step at a time ("a holder stakes 100 SOL; the
  network issues 7 new SOL and routes them here; the non-staker's slice just shrank by that much"). The
  mechanism becomes something the reader *watches happen* rather than something asserted. Round numbers
  keep the arithmetic invisible so the *shape* of the flow stays visible.
- **The interrogated-source / Socratic beat.** When a dense document, paper, or chain of logic must be
  conveyed, break it into a short question-then-answer rhythm ("Who holds the most of these? The treasury.
  What happens when supply rises? The price falls."). The dialogue cadence converts a wall of exposition
  into momentum, and each Q sets up the reader to *want* the next A.
- **Define as you go, in one clause.** Introduce each term the moment it's first needed ("duration — how
  sensitive a price is to a rate change"), never a front-loaded glossary. The reader never hits an
  undefined word, and never reads a definition for a thing they can't yet place.
- **Kinetic verbs for invisible events.** Describe state changes with motion ("tokens flow to," "the
  deposit unlocks," "fees burn") so processes the reader can't see acquire a mental picture.

## 3. Structure that de-risks length
- **Open with a one-paragraph roadmap that names the route.** Tell the reader the path the piece will take
  in order ("first where the new tokens come from, then who quietly pays for them") so the length reads as
  a planned journey, not an open-ended slog.
- **Named section headers that double as a skim-path.** Write headers that, read alone in sequence, trace
  the entire argument, so a drifting reader rejoins anywhere and a skimmer still gets the spine. (A run of
  headers should narrate: setup → the strain → the break → the response → the consequence.)
- **Decompose the intimidating thing** into a few labeled parts handled one at a time, each finished
  before the next begins, so no single section ever feels overwhelming.
- **The scenario fork.** When the outcome is genuinely uncertain, structure the analytical middle as
  explicit branches ("Scenario One… Scenario Two"; "If this, then that — and if I'm wrong, here's the
  other path"). Naming the branches keeps the stakes live: the reader stays to learn which one fires.
- **Mid-piece recap checkpoints.** Every few sections, compress what's established into a short numbered
  list ("to recap how we got here: 1… 2… 3…") so a reader who drifted can re-board without rereading. On
  a long piece these checkpoints are the difference between finishing and bailing.
- **The recursive causal chain.** When explaining *why* something happens, follow the causality one link
  at a time ("this happens because of that, which happens because of this other thing"), so a complex
  outcome arrives as a sequence of small, obvious steps.

## 4. Honest, useful landings
- **The falsifiability close, made concrete and named.** End an analytical piece on a dedicated beat
  (literally "What I'd watch" and "How you'd know I'm wrong") naming the *specific* signal to monitor and
  the two or three things that would break the thesis. It signals intellectual honesty and hands the
  reader a way to keep thinking past the last line. (Not a vague hedge: name the metric and the threshold.)
- **The actionable next-steps close.** Where it fits, end with a concrete "what to do now": the payoff
  that turns an explainer into something the reader can act on, in plain steps rather than a wrap-up.

## 5. The signature strengths (what this author does better than most)
- **Metaphor as architecture:** one organizing image that *titles, recurs through, and bookends* an
  entire long piece. (Distinct from Balaji, whose analogy reframes a whole domain to argue inevitability,
  and from Hotz, whose analogy is a small machine used once and dropped: Hayes's metaphor is a narrative
  *spine* whose whole job is to carry reader attention across length.)
- **Stamina engineering** — roadmap + skim-path headers + recap checkpoints + restated stakes that keep a
  cold reader moving through difficult material to the end.
- **Vivid causality:** forces-as-actors, toy-number walkthroughs, and kinetic verbs that make invisible
  mechanisms legible.
- **Honest, actionable closes:** a named falsifiable signal and concrete next steps instead of a wrap-up.

## 6. Generation guidance (+ worked example, in neutral original voice)
**To apply this craft to a Solana lesson:** open on a concrete scene and extract its principle → state the
stake for the reader as a concrete consequence → give a one-paragraph roadmap that names the route → carry
the same metaphor through labeled, skim-path sections → run one round-number toy example → drop a recap
checkpoint if the piece is long → close with a callback plus "what to watch / how you'd know I'm wrong /
what to do." **Keep the architecture, drop the costume:** stay an affectionate builder-optimist, bullish
on the tech, honest on the risk, never acidic, never trader-cynical, never sneering at the reader or the
ecosystem. Concretize forces as actors *without* casting them as villains. Write it in *your* voice.

**Worked micro-example — where staking rewards come from (economics; opening + spine):**
> Picture a company that pays a dividend, but not out of profits — it pays by printing brand-new shares
> and handing them to shareholders who show up to collect. Show up, and you end up with more shares. Sit
> out, and you still own the same number. But now they're a thinner slice of a bigger pie.
>
> A staking reward is almost exactly that, and the stake for you is direct: it's the difference between
> "7% yield" being real income and being an illusion you're partly funding yourself. So here's the route —
> first where the new tokens actually come from, then who quietly pays for them, then how to tell whether
> the yield is worth it.
>
> When you stake SOL, the network mints fresh SOL and routes it to stakers as a reward. Picture a clean
> example: 1,000 SOL exists, the network issues 70 new ones this year, and you hold 100 staked. You
> collect 7. You're up to 107 of a now-1,070 supply, the same share you started with. The holder who
> *didn't* stake still has their 100, but of a bigger pie. Their slice just thinned. The tokens weren't
> taken from anyone's pocket; they were *created*, and the bill is paid in dilution by everyone who sits
> out. Stake, and you're collecting the new shares. Sit out, and you're the one quietly funding them.

*(Craft used: concrete-scene metaphor as the spine; stake stated as a second-person consequence;
one-paragraph roadmap naming the route; round-number toy example walked one step at a time;
force-as-actor ("the network mints… routes…") with no villain. No profanity, nicknames, trader identity,
or ideology — the engagement engine, in a neutral voice you can make your own.)*

## What this profile deliberately EXCLUDES
Original writing is the goal, so the author's identifiable surface idiolect is **not** captured and should
**not** be reproduced: profanity and crude/sexual imagery, recurring nicknames for public figures, the
autobiographical trader identity (the fund, the positions, the "I aped in"), song-title and lyric
sign-offs, the cosmetic lone-exotic-word essay-title tic (keep the metaphor-organizes-and-titles *craft*;
drop the branding habit), and the anti-fiat / monetary-doom ideology that frames every system as a scam and
every institution as a predator. Keep the narrative architecture; drop the costume. The hardest leak to catch is
**tone**: his native register is trader-cynicism and contempt: the reader as "schmuck," the ecosystem as
a casino, the future as collapse. The system's voice is the opposite: an affectionate builder-optimist.
Lift the metaphor-spine, the stakes, the stamina scaffolding, and the honest close. And render them
bullish on the technology, honest about real risk, and warm toward the reader. Never acidic, never
sneering, never doom.
